FOREWORD
Soil testing in India had its modest beginning in 1955-56 with
the setting up of 24 soil testing laboratories under the overall supervision
and control of the Indian Agricultural Research Institute, New
Delhi, valuable material support as well as technical assistance being
provided by the United States of America through its Technical Co-
operation Mission (later on USAID). As a result of the growing
interest, the programme made rapid strides and today the country is
having one of the world's largest chain of soil testing laboratories, both
static and mobile, operated by Central and State Governments, Agri-
cultural Universities, Research Institutions, Fertilizer Industry as well as
some cooperative and private organisations, with the IARI having
continued to take the responsibility of technical coordination.

for trained personnel, the IARI, in collaboration with the USAID
(TCM) initiated, as early as in 1958, a Short Course Training in
Soil Testing with a view to have a uniform standard in the laboratory
procedures and also to provide a wider perspective in the concepts
of soil testing to the scientists working in the various laboratories.

the Institute has been regularly organising the Refresher Training

COLLECTION AND PREPARATION OF SOIL
SAMPLES FOR ANALYSIS
The importance of having a truly representative sample can be very
well realized from the fact that only a minute fraction of the huge soil
mass of the field is actually used for analysis in the laboratory. While
collecting samples the following aspects should be carefully considered.
The sample must properly represent the area. A field can be
treated as a single sampling unit only if it is appreciably uniform in all
respects. Variation in slope, colour, texture, crop growth and manage-
ment should be taken into account and separate sets of composite
samples need to be collected from each of such area. Recently fertilized
plots, bunds, channels, marshy tracts and spots near trees, wells, compost
piles or other non-representative locations must be carefully avoided
during sampling.
The root penetration of plants is an important consideration for
ascertaining the depth of sampling. Fields growing cereals and other
seasonal crops are normally sampled up to 0-15 cm (generally expressed
as plough layer). For deep rooted crops like sugarcane, horticultural
plants or under dry farming conditions it may be necessary to obtain
samples from different depths or layers.
In case of saline-alkali soils, salt crusts (visible or suspected) on the
soil surface should be sampled separately and the depth of sampling
recorded. Generally the sample may be drawn to the plough depth.
Sampling Procedure
To obtain a composite sample, small portions of soil are to be
collected up to the desired depth (0-15 cm or more) by means of suitable
sampling tools from at least 10 to 15 well distributed spots (from each
sampling unit) after scrapping off the surface litter, if any. When crops
are planted in rows samples can be drawn in between the lines, if not
recently fertilized.
1
The soil collected in this manner should be thoroughly mixed (by
hand) on a clean piece of cloth, polythene sheet or thick paper and the
bulk reduced by quartering and about 500g (1/2 kg) of the composite
sample is retained. The soil must be quickly air dried in shade at room
temperature and put in cloth or polythene bags with suitable description
and identification marks.
Sampling tools
Samples can be drawn with any of these hand implements namely,
(i) soil tube (auger), (ii) screw type auger, (iii) post-hole auger, (iv) kassi
(spade) and (v) khurpi (a common gardening hand tool).
For sampling of soft and moist soil the tube auger, spade or khurpi
is quite satisfactory. A screw type auger may prove more convenient on
hard/dry soil while the post-hole auger is useful for sampling excessively
wet area like rice field. If a spade or khurpi is used, a V-shaped cut may
be first made (up to the plough layer) and a uniformly 1.5 cm (approx.)
thick slice is taken out. Augers are necessary for sampling from lower
depths.
The various steps involved in the proper collection of representative
soil samples in the field and their handling are depicted in figure 1.
Precautions in collection and storing of samples: Care in handling
the soil samples against contamination is extremely important. Special
precautions are necessary if the samples are to be tested for micronutrient
elements. Any possibility of contact with chemicals, fertilizers or manures
must be avoided. Cotton, jute or plastic bags which had previously
been containing fertilizers, salt or lime should not be used at any stage.
Soil samples should preferably be stored in clean cloth or polythene bags.
Glass, porcelain or polythene jars are useful in keeping few selected
samples for long duration. The bags can be re-used only after thorough
washing and drying.
Information sheet: In addition to the location, field number, name
of cultivator and identification marks, relevant information regarding
slope, drainage, irrigation, previous cropping history, fertilizer use etc.
of the field (s) must be recorded and sent alongwith the soil samples.
This is a specific requirement in soil testing work and must be insisted
upon. A specimen of the information sheet to be filled up is given in
appendix. I. This can be partly modified if felt necessary.

Some selected physical, physico-chemical and chemical tests such as
determination of texture, pH (soil reaction), electrical conductivity,
mineralizable nitrogen and available phosphorus and potassium are
usually carried out to evaluate such of the soil properties which are
known to have a direct impact on the productivity of the soil as well as
its fertility status. A qualitative assessment of the free calcium carbonate
content, if any, is also made in case of neutral and alkaline soils.
1. SOIL TEXTURE
The texture is determined by a rapid procedure by feel or rubbing
the soil between the thumb and the fingers. Proficiency is gained through
experience, trial and error and comparison with samples of known
textural class.
Steps for texture determination: A small quantity of the dry soil
is moistened and mixed thoroughly on a glass or porcelain dish so as to
form a soft ball and then worked until stiff and squeezed out between
thumb and fore-finger. The feel to fingers, ease of forming ball, sticki-
ness or grittiness, whether forming soil ribbons or merely crumbling on
squeezing etc. are to be noted. The texture is classified as follows:

The textural class of known soil samples taken for comparison is
correctly ascertained from the percentage distribution of sand, silt and
clay determined according to the international pipette method (Piper
1966) and using the standard triangular diagram.
II. DETERMINATION OF SOIL REACTION (pH)
The pH value which is a measure of the hydrogen (or hydroxyl)
ion activity of the soil-water system indicates whether the soil is acidic,
neutral or alkaline in reaction. Since crop growth suffers much both
under very low (strongly acidic) as well as high (alkali) pH, suitable
reclamation measures become necessary. Presence of neutral soluble
salts (as in saline soils) is not normally reflected in the pH but when in
large excess they tend to suppress the ionic activity.
Instrument
The instrument for pH measurement commonly used is a glass
electrode pH meter with calomel reference electrode including salt
bridge. Most digital pH meters have single (combined) electrode
assembly. The instrument being a potentiometer, the pH scale has to be
calibrated before use with buffer solutions of known pH values.
Since the dissociation/ionic activity increases with temperature the
pH scale of 0 to 14 is valid only for a particular temperature (25°C).
Thus all instruments are provided with temperature setting/correction
knob which must be made use of.
Reagent
Standard buffer solutions: These may be of pH 4.0, 7.0 or 92 in
pure water or in other ranges of expected pH value. In case of buffer
tablets (available commercially) a single piece is to be dissolved in freshly
double distilled water and made up to 100ml. It is necessary to prepare a
fresh buffer after every few days as the solutions do not keep for long. A
0.05M analytical grade (AR) potassium hydrogen phthalate (KHCH₄O4
mol. wt. 204.14) gives a pH of 4.0 (actually 3.97) at 25°C and this can
also be used as a standard buffer. A stock solution of 0.25M is prepared
by dissolving 51.04 g of the pure dry salt in warm water and making up
to 1 litre. Three to four drops of toluene are added to prevent growth of
mould. A dilution of 10 ml of this solution to 50 ml with water gives
0 05M solution.
Procedure for pH measurement
(a) pH in saturated soil paste: This is prepared by adding small
amounts of distilled water to a portion of soil while working with a
spatula. At saturation, the soil paste glistens, flows slightly when the
container is tilted, slides freely and cleanly off the spatula. After mixing,
it should be allowed to stand with a cover above the container for about
an hour. Free water must not collect on the soil surface nor should the
paste stiffen markedly or lose its glistening appearance on standing The
pH meter is to be set for the temperature and calibrated with two buffers,
one in the acidic side and the other in alkaline or neutral range. The
electrodes (glass and calomel or combined) are carefully inserted in the
paste and pH measurement is made. The electrode is moved a little to
ensure removal of water film around it and pH reading is again taken
when it is constant.
(b) Soil to water ratio of 1:2: 20g of soil is taken in a 100 ml
beaker to which 40 ml of distilled water is added. The suspension is
stirred at regular intervals for 30 minutes and the pH is recorded. The
suspension must be stirred well just before the electrodes are immersed
and readings taken.
(c) pH in 0.01M CaCl2 solution : To 20g of soil in a beaker 40 ml
of 0.01M CaCl2 solution (1.470g AR CaCl2.2H2O/litre) is added. The
suspension is shaken thoroughly (30 minutes) and the pH is recorded.
The reading is shifted downwards in comparison to the pH value obtained
in water suspension. With acid soils it normally ranges from 4 to 5 and
7
in calcareous ones between 6.9 and 7.2. The pH of near neutral soil in
CaCl₂ solution is usually around 6.
N.B. Before each determination the electrodes must be washed with
a jet of distilled water and water removed from the surface with a piece
of filter or blotting paper.
III. MEASUREMENT OF ELECTRICAL CONDUCTIVITY
Since ions are the carrier of electricity, the electrical conductivity
(EC) of the soil-water system rises according to the content of soluble
salts in the soil giving rise to more of ion pairs on dissociation as it
happens in case of a dilute solution. Thus the measurement of EC can be
directly related to the soluble salts concentration of the soil at any
particular temperature.
Instrument
The apparatus for measuring electrical conductivity consists of an
AC salt bridge or electrical resistance bridge and conductivity cell having
electrodes coated with platinum black. The instrument is also available
as an already calibrated assembly (solubridge) for giving directly the
conductivity of solutions in millimhos per cm at 25°C.
Reagent
Standard potassium chloride solution: 0.7456g of dry reagent grade
potassium chloride is dissolved in freshly prepared double distilled water
and made to one litre. At 25°C it gives an electrical conductivity of
1411.8×10-6 (0.0014118) mhos/cm or 1.41 mmhos/cm. The conductivity
bridge is to be calibrated and cell constant determined with the help of
this solution. Even if the scale is marked directly in mmhos/cm (as in
solubridge) it is necessary to check and calibrate the instrument with the
KCl solution.
Procedure
20g of soil is shaken intermittently with 40 ml of distilled water in
a 150 ml Erlenmeyer (conical) flask for one hour and allowed to stand.
Alternatively, the clear extract after pH determination can be used for
electrical conductivity measurement. The conductivity of the super-
natant liquid is determined with the help of the salt (conductivity) bridge.
The measurement of EC (expressed in mmhos/cm) is to be adjusted for
a known temperature (usually 25°C) of the solution by setting the knob
provided for this purpose.
Test for calcium carbonate
A qualitative rapid test for free calcium carbonate (CaCO3) is made
by observing the intensity of effervescence (due to CO2 evolution) when
about 10ml of dilute hydrochloric acid (1+3) is added to a small quantity
(about 5g) of air dry soil taken on a watch glass. The amount of CaCO3
present in the soil can be broadly categorized as (i) traces, (ii) low,
(iii) medium (iv) high and (v) very high on the basis of the effervescence.
For soils showing high and very high degree of effervescence a
separate quantitative assay can be made by reacting a known weight of
sample with a known volume of excess standard (N/2 or N/5) HCl and
back titration (after warming and shaking) of unreacted acid with stan-
dard alkali (Piper 1966). The nett quantity of acid consumed can be
attributed to the presence of free calcium carbonate.
IV. BIOLOGICAL AND CHEMICAL METHODS FOR ESTIMA-
TION OF AVAILABLE NITROGEN
The major part (more than 90%) of soil nitrogen exists in complex
combination in the organic matter (humus) fraction. It becomes available
to crops after breakdown to simple forms followed by mineralization.
This type of transformation is mostly biological in nature. Therefore,
methods involving the determination of mineral forms of nitrogen
(NH4-N and NO3-N) produced on incubation of soil under aerobic and
anaerobic conditions are generally followed. These are considered to be
quite satisfactory as index of nitrogen availability in arable soils.
Incubation under waterlogged conditions may be preferred over aerobic
incubation. Besides this, estimation of CO2 produced or evaluation of the
microbial growth or pigment production on incubation of soil samples
with nitrogen-free readily decomposable organic material or nutrient
media and microbial cultures, is also carried out.
The chemical methods for determination of soil nitrogen availability
involve either direct or indirect estimation of total N or measurement of
a fraction of easily hydrolysable nitrogen by employing solutions of
dilute acids or alkali. Distillation with alkaline potassium permanganate
solution has often been adopted for estimating the readily oxidisable
and reactive forms of soil nitrogen.
One of the most widely used rapid soil testing techniques for the
assessment of available nitrogen is based upon the estimation of readily
oxidisable organic carbon which roughly represents 58% of the soil

Procedure: 10g of air-dry soil (sieved through 2 mm) is intimately

At the end of this period, the stopper is removed and 100 ml of 2N KCI
is added and the neck of the bottle is fitted back with a solid rubber
stopper. The suspension is shaken for one hour on a mechanical shaker
and filtered (Whatman No.1). 20 ml aliquot of the clear filtrate is taken

1964)
(Ammonia released under waterlogged conditions)
Procedure for micro estimation:
12.5 ± 1.0 ml of distilled water is
placed in a 16 mm × 150 mm test tube or 150 ml conical flask and 5g of

obtained after incubation.
Macro estimation: For this purpose, 20g of soil is mixed with
equal weight of quartz sand in 100 ml conical flask. The soil is submerg-
ed with distilled water to a level of 2 cm above the surface. It is incu-
bated for one week at 35°C. Thereafter the contents are leached with
200ml of IN NaCl and ammonia (displaced) is estimated by distilling with
freshly ignited MgO.
The procedure has been found suitable as an index of nitrogen
availability for rice, bajra (pearl millet) and wheat and also as a single
extractant for the simultaneous extraction of NPK for rice.

V. ESTIMATION OF ORGANIC CARBON

Phosphorus in soil occurs as orthophosphate in several forms and
combinations and only a small fraction of the total amount present may

proposed which extract variable quantities of phosphorus.

forms of adsorbed and chemically bound phosphate in the soil system.
The extractants have been developed either from the concept of
simulating the action of plant roots or to obtain a suitable measure of
the readily soluble inorganic forms to represent the plant available soil
phosphorus. Thus, the estimation of specific categories of soil Pconsi-

reactive (high specific surface) form of P is extracted from the phosphates
of iron, aluminium and calcium present in the soil.
In soil testing work, the available phosphorus content is readily
determined by extraction with suitable reagents according to specified
soil to solution ratio and time of shaking. In the filtered extract,
phosphorus is estimated colorimetrically by adding ammonium molybdate
and thereafter reducing the molybdenum-phosphate complex in acidic
medium. The intensity of blue colour on reduction provides a measure
for the concentration of P in the test solution. The two extractants and
procedures normally used are given below.
1. Olsen's method (Olsen et al. 1954)
The extracting reagent for Olsen's P is 0.5 molar sodium bicarbo-
nate (pH 8.5) prepared by dissolving 42.0g of NaHCO3 (laboratory
reagent) in distilled water to give one litre of the solution. The pH is
adjusted to 8.5 with small quantities of 10% NaOH. The method has been
found widely applicable in slightly acid, neutral and calcareous soils.
To 2.5g of soil in 100 ml conical flask a little of Darco G 60 or
equivalent grade of activated carbon (free of phosphorus) is added
followed by 50 ml of Olsen's reagent. A blank is to be run without soil.
The flasks are shaken for 30 minutes on a platform type shaker and the
contents filtered immediately through dry filter paper (Whatman No.1)
into clean and dry beakers or vials. In the filtrate phosphorus is

procedure.
Note: The activated carbon (even if marked phosphorus-free) is likely to contain
traces of P which have to be removed by repeated washings with Olsen's
reagent followed by warm distilled water. The material should test free of
phosphorus when extracted with Olsen's reagent. The sodium bicarbonate
should also be tested free from any phosphate contamination.
2. Bray's No. 1 method (Bray and Kurtz 1945)
This procedure is primarily meant for soils which are moderately

VIII. AVAILABLE POTASSIUM DETERMINATION
The term available potassium incorporates both exchangeable and
water soluble forms of the nutrient present in soil. The readily exchage-
able plus water soluble K is determined in the neutral normal ammonium
acetate extract of soil. The ammonium ion provides a sharp and rapid
separation from exchange complex while other cations bring about a
gradual replacement of either lesser or greater amount of K which gene-
rally increases with the period of contact. The estimation of potassium
in the extract is carried out with the help of flame photometer. Chemical
methods being rather elaborate and time-consuming are not suitable for
soil testing purpose.
Non-exchangeable K (not lattice-bound) is attributed to contribute
appreciably towards the potassium available to crops. The most
commonly employed extractants are the strong inorganic acids like 6N
H₂SO₄, hot IN HCl and boiling IN HNO3. The procedure for determi-
nation involves either the prior removal of exchangeable K or conditions
are made sufficiently vigorous to extract both exchangeable (including
water soluble) and a portion of non-exchangeable forms from which
the former is subtracted.

XI. PHOSPHORUS FIXING CHARACTERISTICS OF SOIL
Soils vary appreciably in their phosphorus fixing capacity, those
dominating in sesquioxides and kaolinitic type of clays having tendency
to fix or immobilize larger amounts of phosphorus from the soluble
phase. The phosphorus fixing capacity can be experimentally determined
as per procedure outlined by Waugh and Fitts (1966). A double cell
photo-electric colorimeter is needed for phosphorus estimation.
Reagent
Standard phosphorus solution: A stock solution of 2500 ppm of P
is prepared by dissolving 9.440g of pure anhydrous monocalcium phos-
Pro, CL (HP)dallel wir and made to one litre. (Old stock
25
of MCP may not completely go into solution and should not be used).
It is desirable to check the concentration of P by Dickman and Bray's
method (1940) after appropriate dilution. Out of this, solutions of
different concentrations are prepared (by dilution) such that by adding
one ml of each to 2.0g of soil the resultant concentration of added P
would be 0, 25, 50, 75, 100, 125, 250, 375 and 500 ppm.
Procedure
2g of soil is weighed out (in duplicate) in separate 100 ml conical
flasks. One ml of the solutions of different concentrations of P are added
to each so that they cover the range from 0 to 500 ppm as stated above.
The flasks are plugged with cotton wool and allowed to incubate for 96
hours at room temperature and thereafter the phosphorus is extracted by
Olsen's or Bray's method and estimated colorimetrically (Item VII) as
the case may be.
The results of extracted P are plotted (Y-axis) on the graph sheet
against added P (X-axis). The curve is examined carefully and the point
where there is an abrupt increase in extractable P is located. From this
inflexion point (on the curve) the corresponding value of X is found out
i.e., approximate amount of P required to be added to more than over-
come the effect of fixation. If the fixation curve is linear its shape may
be considered to work out the amount of P to be applied which would
take into account the fixing capacity.

NIII. SOIL TEST FOR AVAILABLE SULPHUR
Chemical tests for estimation of available sulphur are of recent
interest in soil testing work particularly in some reported sulphur
deficient areas and for certain crops whose requirement of this nutrient
is high enough, often exceeding that of phosphorus.
Of several extracting procedures suggested for available sulphur
determination, starting from distilled water for dissolving out the sulphate
form to different chemical extractants, the use of phosphate solution has
often been preferred for screening the soil for its sulphur fertility status.
A number of other reagents have also been tried such as, Olsen's,
29

Morgan's and Bray's, normal magnesium acetate and 0.15 per cent
calcium chloride solution.
As regards the estimation of sulphur in the extract turbidimetric
procedure is being most widely followed. The extent of turbidity due to
fine suspension of barium sulphate formed by reacting sulphate ions with
barium chloride in solution can be accurately measured in the nephelo-
meter (working on the principle of scattered light) but within the range
of low concentration spectrophotometer or even colorimeter can work
fairly satisfactorily.
Recently, a new colorimetric method of sulphur estimation has been
developed in the Soil Testing Laboratory of the I.A.R.I., New Delhi,
based on the earlier suggestion of Nemeth (1964). In this procedure,
barium chromate reacting with sulphate in solution precipitates the
latter as BaSO, releasing equivalent quantity of chromate ion which is
subjected to colorimetric measurement. The method can work quite
well for a variety of extractants except those containing phosphate
because when the reaction of the medium is changed from acidic to
alkaline for removing unreacted barium chromate, barium phosphate
also gets precipitated thus affecting the final colour intensity.

Water is the most universal solvent and its all naturally occurring
sources are bound to contain variable quantities of dissolved solids and
gases. Sometimes, suspended and colloidal organic and inorganic
materials occur as well. Different types of laboratory tests of water
are carried out depending on its use from the point of view of industry,
public health or agriculture. Thus a water considered to be of poor
quality for industrial or domestic purpose may be quite suitable for
irrigation in which case total soluble salts, nature and concentration of
various ions and particularly the proportion of the divalent and monova
lent cations are of specific relevance in judging the water quality.

polythene bottles (stoppered) which should preferably be transparent
The container must be thoroughly cleaned before use and should be
rinsed 3 to 4 times with the water from which the sample is to be drawn.
Brand new bottles are better to avoid and when used the cleaning and
washing have to be more rigorous after keeping these filled with water
for one or two days.

4. FLAME PHOTOMETRY
When a substance is heated, it emits radiant energy. The emission
becomes stronger with greater excitation of the molecules/atoms. This
energy (electromagnetic radiation) is composed of radiations of different
wavelengths; the assemblage of radiation is the emission spectrum of
the substance. There are three kinds of emission spectra :
(i) Continuous spectrum, given out by incandescent solids, consist-
ing of continuous wavelength range, where individual lines are
absent;
(ii) Band spectrum, emitted by excited molecules, consisting of
individual bands which are actually composed of groups of lines
very close to one another; and
(iii) Line spectrum, originating from excited atoms or atomic ions
(which exclude polyatomic ions or radicals). These spectra
consist of distinct and often widely spaced lines.
Analytical flame photometry is based on the measurement of the
intensity of the characteristic line emission of the element to be deter-
mined (Jackson 1973). When a solution of a salt is sprayed into a flame
(acetylene, propane or liquefied petroleum gas) the salt gets separated
into its component atoms because of the high temperature. The energy
provided by the flame excites the atoms to higher energy levels. Actually,
the orbital electrons are shifted to higher planes from their normal orienta-
tion. When the electrons return back to the ground or unexcited state,
they emit their characteristic radiation. Since the excitation can be to
different levels, light (electromagnetic radiation) of several wavelengths
can be emitted. However, the intensity of the wavelength corresponding
to the most probable transition will be the highest. For each element,
such characteristic lines have already been very well identified.
58
Each individual atom emits one quantum of radiation. Therefore,
the intensity of radiation emitting from the flame (which must be other-
wise steady and non-luminous) will be proportional to the number of
atoms in the flame, that is, to the concentration of the 
particular element
in the flame. This concentration is, in turn, directly related 
to the
content of the element in the test solution.
The instrumental set-up for flame photometric analysis consists of
three parts:
(a) Atomization assembly, comprising a nebulizer (atomizer) to
produce a fine mist from the solution and a burner 
which
vaporizes the analyte (in atomized form);
(b) Monochromation system (filter, prism) that separates out the
analytical wavelength from the other radiations; and
(c) Photometric system for measuring the intensity of the emitted
radiation.
Analytical Procedure
A series of standard solutions are prepared and the intensity of
emission determined for each concentration after zero 
setting of the
blank. A calibration curve is drawn from these 
data.
The intensity of emission from the test solution(s) is measured
simultaneously and the concentration of the element 
is read from the

should be made use of.

Care and maintenance: Although a very sensitive instrument, flame
photometers are usually fairly sturdy and give trouble-free service for
years if handled properly. The burner assembly has two external conn-
ections, one leading in gas directly to the burner and the other taking
compressed air through a spraying nozzle (atomizer). The air stream
carries with it to the burner flame a spray of the solution under test. If
any chocking or disconnection takes place it should be located and can
be set right easily. The gas and air pressure must remain steady during
the operation of the instrument and the connections and regulators
should be checked before use. It is of utmost importance that only very
clear solutions/extracts are fed in to avoid any possible chocking of the
capillary. Burners of flame photometers should be cleaned periodically.
While closing down, the gas supply should be first turned off and then
the compressed air line. When large salt concentrations or strong acid
solutions are to be fed in (which should be avoided as far as possible)
distilled water should be run for some time and the burner cleaned
immediately after use. The galvanometer assembly must not be touched
and any repairs should be carried out at a competent workshop.
5. ATOMIC ABSORPTION SPECTROPHOTOMETRY
The principle behind analytical atomic absorption spectrophoto-
metry (AAS) is the measurement of the absorption of light (radiant)
energy by free atoms in the ground state, and its quantitative correlation
with the concentration of the analyte in solution.
Absorption of light by atoms: When a solution of a salt is aspirated
into a flame the solute molecules dissociate into neutral atoms (fund-
amental, ground or unexcited state) and excited neutral atoms and ions.
In the present day atomic absorption spectrometry one is concerned with
the fundamental state atoms only. The number of fundamental state
atoms in the flame (acetylene, propane, LPG) is far in excess of that of
excited ones. (Excited atoms are responsible for emission of radiations
which are measured in analytical flame photometry, as described earlier
under item 4).
When light beam of the appropriate wavelength is passed through
the cloud of atoms in the flame the incident radiation is subjected to
absorption by the neutral fundamental state atoms. Obviously, these
atoms now reach the excited state. The absorption follows the Beer-
Lambert law, i.e., the absorbance is proportional to the number of
ground state atoms (Willard et al. 1965). It must be noted that this
relationship holds good only for the specific (resonance) wavelength
(similar to absorption spectrophotometry of solutions where it is of
course, molecular absorption).
The atoms of the salt solution excited by absorption of the incident
radiation, emit radiation when they return back to the ground state.
Since the wavelength of this radiation is not the same as that of the
incident light beam (absorbed radiation), this emission will interfere with
absorbance measurement. In AAS this difficulty is obviated by employing
a light source of a specific pulse frequency and matching it with an AC
amplifier which will respond to signals of that particular frequency only
(Willard et al. 1965). Thus, the emitted radiation (which is a continuous
one) will not be measured by the photometric system.
Instrumentation
The component systems of an AAS unit are:
(i) Light source
(ii) Atomization assembly (including burner)
61
60
(iii) Monochromator, and
(iv) Detector and readout assembly.
Light source: The lamp used should emit the resonance radiation
of the element the content of which is to be determined. It should be
sufficiently intense and stable to permit reliable measurement and must
have a low background emission (low intensity of interfering lines).
Hollow cathode lamps with the element to be analyzed as the
cathode and filled with an inert gas at low pressure, usually constitute
the light source. These give the characteristic emission spectrum of the
cathode material. This spectrum contains the resonance wavelength
radiation along with other light radiations.
Atomization system: The atomization assembly consists of a
nebulizer to produce uniformly fine fog of droplets from the solution to
be analyzed, and a burner. The unit is more or less the same as that
used in flame photometry, except that the burner has a long and narrow
slot at the top (like a fish-tail burner) so that the flame which is analogous
to the absorption cell in colorimetry, provides a long absorption path
for the incident radiation. The fuel-oxidant system used may be acety-
lene-air, acetylene-oxygen, acetylene-nitrous oxide, hydrogen-air,
hydrogen-oxygen, propane-air, propane-oxygen or LPG-oxygen/air.
Monochromator: The monochromator enables the isolation of the
resonance wavelength from other radiations before measurement of inten-
sity. A diffraction grating or prism is normally used. Filters are
usually not adequate for complete monochromation.
Detection and readout system: This unit consists of a photo-sensi-
tive component (photo-multipliers are commonly employed), an AC
amplifier, and a sensitive galvanometer to measure the current.
Analytical Procedure
The absorbance values corresponding to known standard solutions
are recorded after necessary blank setting and a calibration curve pre-
pared. The absorbance of the test solution is determined and the con-
centration read off from the calibration curve.
The calibration must be done for each set of analysis since in
practice it is not possible to reproduce exactly the nebulization and flame
conditions.

developed with variable degree of success.

Important points regarding operation and maintenance of the atomic
absorption spectrophotometer: Being one of the quite sophisticated, high
precision and fairly expensive type of measuring instrument, the AAS,
requires very careful handling and maintenance.
The light source is a critical component. There should not be any
erratic fluctuations in the intensity. Any fluctuations in the line voltage
must be taken care of. Lamps are generally operated at low currents to
improve linearity of response and maintain narrow emission lines, as
much as possible.
While extinguishing the flame the fuel supply should be first turned
off before stopping the oxygen or air line. The oxygen (or air) and fuel
pressure must be adjusted to the value recommended in the instruction
manual furnished by the manufacturer. The width of the slit is usually
set at 0.02-0.05 mm as instructed in the working manual. The wavelength
should also be set properly for each determination by making use of the
relevant components like filter photometer monochromator.

The various aspects to be considered for optimum working con-
ditions are the emission intensity, steady rate of atomization, the
resolution of the spectral lines (or bands) from each other and from
adjacent spectral features of other sample constituents, the magnitude
of the background radiation, and steadiness of readings.
The AAS can give reliable and trouble-free service only with careful
handling. Erratic readings may come from fluctuations in gas and air pre-
ssure and electric supply or the lamps becoming weak due to prolonged
use. If something goes wrong, the instrument should never be opened

the repairing job.

A fairly large number of replicated experiments have been con-
ducted on farmers' fields in the Union Territory of Delhi to evaluate
these procedures and a few examples of the results obtained with wheat
crop are cited in tables 1 and 2 (Bajaj5).
Fertilizer recommendations on soil test basis have proved definitely
more profitable than ad-hoc application of standard doses (according to
the general package of practices). The response ratio was still higher
where adjustment equation for economic yield by multiple regression
technique was adopted (Table 1).
